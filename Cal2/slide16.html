A power series is a series of the form \[\sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\] where \(c_k, a\) are real numbers.

For the previous series, describe \(a, c_k\).

Theorem:
Given a power series \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\) there are three possibilities:

  1. \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\) converges for all values of \(x\).
  2. \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\) converges only when \(x=a\).
  3. There is a \(R > 0\) such that \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\) converges when \(|x-a| < R\).

In the last case, we test seperately if \(x= a-R, a+R\) converges.

The values of \(x\) for which \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k\) converges is called the interval of convergence.
