Suppose a function had two power series forms \[f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_kx^k = \sum_{k=0}^\infty b_kx^k.\]

What must be true about \(\displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_kx^k - \sum_{k=0}^\infty b_kx^k\) as a function of \(x\)?

What can we say when we compare \(c_k, b_k\)?

If \(f(x) = \displaystyle \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k(x-a)^k \) on some interval of convergence \(I\), then \(\displaystyle c_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}\)

We call \(\displaystyle f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\) the Taylor Series of \(f(x)\) centered at \(a\).

We call \(\displaystyle f_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k\) the \(n\)th degree Taylor Polynomial of \(f(x)\).

If \(a=0\), we call the Taylor Series a Maclaurin Series for historical reasons.

Let's find a Taylor Series centered at \(0\) for \(sin(x)\). Let \(f(x)=\sin(x)\)

Find \(f(0), f'(0), f''(0), f'''(0), f''''(0)\).

We have that \[ \sin(x) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^?x^{2k+1}}{(?)!} \]
