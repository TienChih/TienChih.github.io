Given a vector \(\mathbf{a}=\langle a_1, a_2\rangle\) and scalars \(k, l\), we have \((k+l)\mathbf{a} = k\mathbf{a}+l\mathbf{a}\).

Note that by the definition of scalar products: \[(k+l)\mathbf{a} = (k+l)\langle a_1, a_2\rangle=\langle(k+1)a_1, (k+l)a_2\rangle.\]

Then by distributivity of real-number products and sums: \[\langle (k+1)a_1, (k+l)a_2\rangle=\langle ka_1+la_1, ka_2+la_2\rangle.\]

Then, by the definition of vector addition: \[\langle ka_1+la_1, ka_2+la_2\rangle=\langle ka_1, ka_2\rangle+\langle la_1, la_2 \rangle.\]

Finally, by definition of scalar products: \[\langle ka_1, ka_2\rangle + \langle la_1, la_2\rangle =k\langle a_1, a_2\rangle+l\langle a_1,a_2\rangle=k\mathbf{a}+l\mathbf{a}.\]

Given a vectors \(\mathbf{a}=\langle a_1, a_2\rangle, \mathbf{b}=\langle b_1, b_2\rangle\) and scalar \(k\), prove that \(k(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) = k\mathbf{a}+k\mathbf{b}\).

Some vector arithmetic facts!

  • \(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{a}\).
  • \((\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b})+\mathbf{c}=\mathbf{a}+(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{c})\).
  • \(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{0}=\mathbf{a}\).
  • \(k(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b})=k\mathbf{a}+k\mathbf{b}\).
  • \((k+l)\mathbf{a}=k\mathbf{a}+l\mathbf{a}\).
  • \((kl)\mathbf{a}=(lk)\mathbf{a}\).
